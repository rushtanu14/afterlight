# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Households, caregivers, and community members preparing for wildfire disruptions before an active emergency. They need a calm way to assign responsibilities, expose unresolved coordination gaps, rehearse a plan, and preserve lessons from attributable historical incidents without entering exact addresses or sensitive household details.

## Product Purpose

Afterlight separates current public-source monitoring from historical disaster learning. It turns source-backed historical lessons and non-sensitive household constraints into a practical, device-local drill artifact that families can review, print, and practice before an incident. Success means the household leaves with concrete coarse decisions, primary and backup responsibility, visible unresolved questions, and a dated practice record, not a claim that anyone is safe or ready.

## Brand Personality

Sober, documentary, dependable. The interface should create calm focus and respect the gravity of real disasters without sensational imagery, gamification, or authoritative emergency language.

## Anti-references

- Generic alert dashboards that imply live operational authority.
- Route planners, countdown timers, risk scores, and prediction surfaces.
- Form-heavy preparedness checklists with no ownership, provenance, or printable outcome.
- Cinematic styling that overwhelms evidence, accessibility, or task completion.
- Green completion states that resemble safety certification.

## Design Principles

1. Convert evidence into assigned practice, not advice for an active incident.
2. Make unresolved questions more visible than completion theater.
3. Design backward from a concise printable household drill card.
4. Keep current signals, historical lessons, and household planning visibly separate.
5. Preserve privacy through coarse locations, bounded local storage, and explicit data limits.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG 2.2 AA. Preserve keyboard access, visible focus, semantic labels, clear error and empty states, readable contrast, responsive layouts down to 320px, and full reduced-motion equivalence. Avoid color-only status communication and avoid language that assumes every household has a car, smartphone, or able-bodied members.
